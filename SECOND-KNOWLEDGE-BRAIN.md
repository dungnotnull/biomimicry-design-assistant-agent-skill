## Core Concepts & Frameworks

### What Is Biomimicry?
Biomimicry (from Greek *bios* = life, *mimesis* = imitation) is a design discipline that seeks sustainable solutions to human challenges by emulating nature's time-tested patterns and strategies. Coined by Janine Benyus in her 1997 book *Biomimicry: Innovation Inspired by Nature*, the field rests on the premise that nature has already solved most engineering problems we face — the key is to find the right biological analogy and translate it rigorously into engineering terms.

### The Three Levels of Biomimicry
1. **Form/Organism:** Copying the shape or material of a natural structure (e.g., lotus leaf texture)
2. **Process/Behavior:** Emulating how organisms perform a function (e.g., spider spinning silk)
3. **Ecosystem/System:** Learning from how entire ecosystems self-regulate (e.g., closed-loop material cycling)

### AskNature Functional Taxonomy
AskNature organizes biological strategies by the function they perform, not by the organism. Key top-level function categories:
- **Attach** — temporary/permanent adhesion, fastening
- **Maintain Physical Integrity** — structural support, load distribution, self-repair
- **Protect from Biotic Threats** — antimicrobial, anti-fouling, camouflage
- **Move** — locomotion, fluid transport, vibration
- **Process Matter and Energy** — thermal regulation, photosynthesis analogs, energy storage
- **Sense and Respond** — sensing, signaling, actuation
- **Maintain Homeostasis** — pH buffering, temperature stability, water management

## Biomimicry 3.8 Life's Principles (Janine Benyus)

The Life's Principles are 6 categories of strategies that characterize life as a whole. Every proposed biomimicry design should be evaluated against these principles.

### Category 1: Evolve to Survive
- Replicate strategies that work
- Integrate the unexpected
- Reshuffle information
- Embody resilience through variation, redundancy, and decentralization

### Category 2: Adapt to Changing Conditions
- Maintain integrity through self-renewal
- Embody resilience through variation, redundancy, and decentralization
- Incorporate diversity
- Use feedback loops to regulate internal processes

### Category 3: Be Locally Attuned and Responsive
- Use readily available materials and energy
- Use chemistry and materials that are safe for living systems
- Build from the bottom up
- Combine modular and nested components

### Category 4: Use Life-Friendly Chemistry
- Break down products into benign constituents
- Build selectively with a small subset of elements
- Do chemistry in water

### Category 5: Be Resource-Efficient (Material and Energy)
- Use multi-functional design
- Use low energy processes
- Recycle all materials
- Fit form to function

### Category 6: Integrate Development with Growth
- Self-organize
- Build complexity from simple components
- Induce cooperation through competition

## State-of-the-Art Methods & Tools

### Computational Biomimicry
- **BioM3 (Biologically Inspired Concept Generation):** Georgetown/Georgia Tech tool for mapping functional requirements to biological analogies using ontology-driven search
- **DANE (Design by Analogy to Nature Engine):** Georgia Tech automated analogy retrieval from combined engineering + biology corpus
- **SAPPhIRE Model:** Structure-Action-Phenomenon-Function model for multi-level biomimicry analogy alignment
- **Functional Basis Ontology:** Shared vocabulary for bridging engineering function descriptions with AskNature taxonomy

### Digital Fabrication for Bio-Inspired Structures
- **Multi-material 3D printing (Stratasys Polyjet):** Enables gradient stiffness structures (tendon-to-bone transition)
- **Two-photon lithography:** Sub-micron feature resolution for gecko setal arrays and lotus micro-textures
- **Electrospinning:** Nanofibrous scaffolds mimicking spider silk and extracellular matrix
- **Soft lithography (PDMS):** Replicating biological microstructures (shark denticles, insect eyes)
- **4D printing with shape-memory polymers:** Mimicking plant hygroscopic movement

### Simulation & Modeling
- **Topology optimization (SIMP method):** Convergent to bone trabecular architecture under similar loading conditions
- **Cellular automata for branching patterns:** Simulates tree branching, leaf venation, and coral growth
- **Agent-based modeling:** Simulates flocking, ant colony optimization, and termite construction behavior
- **MD simulation for protein folding:** Models silk fibroin and keratin mechanical properties

## Analytical Frameworks

### Biomimicry 3.8 Life's Principles (Primary Framework)
- **Authority:** Janine Benyus, Biomimicry Guild
- **Use:** Sustainability compliance gate — all proposed designs evaluated against 6 principle categories
- **Scoring:** 6 categories × (Aligns / Partially Aligns / Does Not Align) = 18-cell compliance matrix

### AskNature Functional Taxonomy (Search Framework)
- **Authority:** Biomimicry Institute
- **Use:** Primary search vocabulary for identifying biological analogies by function
- **Scoring:** Function alignment score (0–10) in sub-analogy-mapper

### TRIZ Bio-Inspired Principles (Innovation Framework)
- **Authority:** Genrich Altshuller (TRIZ); adapted for bio-inspired use by Vincent et al. (2006)
- **Use:** Cross-domain inventive principle identification; reduces analogical leap difficulty
- **Scoring:** TRIZ principle match (yes/no + principle name) per biological analogy

### Biologically Inspired Design (BID) Methodology (Process Framework)
- **Authority:** Ashok Goel, Georgia Tech; Chakrabarti (Springer 2014)
- **Use:** Systematic multi-level analogy retrieval and mapping process
- **Scoring:** Analogy strength rating using SAPPhIRE model structure-function levels

### Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) Comparison (Sustainability Framework)
- **Authority:** ISO 14040/14044; ecoinvent database
- **Use:** Compare bio-inspired design vs. conventional alternative on embodied energy, carbon footprint, end-of-life
- **Scoring:** Functional unit-based comparison (e.g., kg CO2-eq per unit performance)
